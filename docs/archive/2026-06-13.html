1. U.S. Pulls Anthropic's Fable 5 and Mythos 5

[policy, ai]

Last 24h: Anthropic disabled Fable 5 and Mythos 5 for every user worldwide on June 13 after a federal order barred foreign nationals from the models.

The Trump administration ordered Anthropic at 5:21 p.m. ET to block all foreign nationals, inside or outside the country, from its two most advanced models, citing national security and a specific jailbreak. Anthropic complied by taking both offline globally and disputes the basis, calling the jailbreak narrow and the underlying capability widely available elsewhere. Dark Reading frames Mythos 5 as an upgrade over the preview and Fable 5 as Mythos made safe for general use. The suspension strands paying customers everywhere while the company contests the directive.

2. Google Sues Chinese Gemini-Powered Smishing Network

[ai, phishing]

Last 24h: Google filed suit on June 12 against Outsider Enterprise, a China-based network it accuses of weaponizing Gemini to flood Americans with phishing texts.

Google alleges the group runs a phishing-as-a-service kit called Outsider and used its Gemini AI agent to generate convincing scam infrastructure. The operation has hit hundreds of thousands of victims, caused losses in the millions, and ties to more than 9,000 fake sites and a million fraudulent URLs. Google's lawsuit aims to dismantle the infrastructure and signals a pattern of criminals using AI to make fraud more believable. Defenders should expect higher-quality smishing lures and tune filtering accordingly.

Vulnerabilities and Exploits

3. Oracle PeopleSoft Zero-Day Loots Universities

[zero-day, breach]

Last 24h: Researchers disclosed a critical PeopleSoft zero-day that ShinyHunters exploited to steal gigabytes from hundreds of organizations, hitting American universities hardest.

A maximum-severity flaw in Oracle-owned PeopleSoft enterprise software let attackers reach into ERP deployments and pull out large volumes of data. The ShinyHunters extortion crew capitalized fast, disproportionately compromising U.S. higher education, which leans heavily on the platform for student and financial records. Affected institutions should patch immediately, hunt for signs of bulk data export, and assume credential exposure. Ars Technica rates the bug about as critical as vulnerabilities come.

Sources: Dark Reading · Ars Technica Security

4. More Than 400 Arch Linux AUR Packages Hijacked

[supply-chain, malware]

Last 24h: Attackers seized over 400 packages in the Arch User Repository this week and rewrote their build scripts to drop a credential stealer and rootkit.

The Arch User Repository, Arch Linux's community-maintained package collection, saw more than 400 entries weaponized to install a Rust infostealer on any machine that built them. The malware harvests developer secrets and access tokens, and when it runs as root it loads an eBPF rootkit to hide itself. Developers who built affected packages should rotate every credential and API key, rebuild from clean sources, and inspect for kernel-level persistence. The incident underscores how community package channels carry trust that attackers can abuse at scale.

Sources: The Hacker News · BleepingComputer

Nation-State Activity

5. Velvet Ant Backdoors Linux Login for Nearly a Decade

[apt, nation-state]

Last 24h: Sygnia revealed that the China-nexus group Velvet Ant hid inside a target's Linux login system for close to ten years.

Rather than camping on closely watched laptops and servers, Velvet Ant backdoored the PAM and OpenSSH components that decide who may sign in, planting access where routine cleanup never looked. Sygnia, which tracks the group, found the implants survived because defenders rarely audit the authentication layer itself. Organizations should verify the integrity of PAM modules and SSH binaries, watch for unexplained authentication paths, and treat long-lived Linux infrastructure as a prime persistence target. The case shows nation-state crews investing in dwell time measured in years.

Sources: The Hacker News

Data Breaches

6. Record Breach Penalties Hit Coupang and 23andMe

[breach, policy]

Last 24h: South Korea fined Coupang a record $409 million over a data breach the same day a bankruptcy administrator approved a $47 million fund for 23andMe breach victims.

South Korea's privacy commission levied its largest-ever data-breach penalty against e-commerce giant Coupang, surpassing the $88.8 million it fined SK Telecom earlier this year. Separately, a U.S. bankruptcy administrator approved a $47 million settlement for roughly 7 million 23andMe customers whose genetic data hackers stole starting in April 2023 and posted on the dark web. Together the actions show regulators and courts attaching real money to lost customer data. Companies holding sensitive personal records face mounting financial exposure when controls fail.

Sources: The Record · The Record

Policy and Regulation

7. Section 702 Surveillance Program Set to Lapse

[policy, surveillance]

Last 24h: Section 702 of the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act is poised to expire after a legislative deadlock, its first lapse since 2008.

Section 702 authorizes a major U.S. foreign-intelligence collection program, and congressional gridlock now threatens to let it expire for the first time in its history. Intelligence agencies rely on the authority to gather communications of foreign targets, and a lapse forces a scramble over how ongoing collection continues. Privacy advocates and surveillance hawks have long fought over reauthorization terms, and the deadlock leaves the program's future unsettled. Organizations tied to federal intelligence sharing should track how a gap affects threat reporting.
