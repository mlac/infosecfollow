1. Anthropic Pulls Fable 5 and Mythos 5

[ai, policy]

Last 24h: Anthropic disabled Claude Fable 5 and Mythos 5 worldwide after the US government ordered it to block all foreign nationals from both models, citing national security.

The Trump administration's directive arrived at 5:21 p.m. ET and bars every foreign national from the two models, whether inside or outside the US, so Anthropic suspended access globally for all users. The company complies but disputes the basis, calling the cited jailbreak narrow and the same capability widely available elsewhere. Dark Reading frames Mythos 5 as an upgrade over Mythos Preview and Fable 5 as Mythos made safe for general use.

Vulnerabilities and Exploits

2. Oracle PeopleSoft Zero-Day

[zero-day, breach]

Last 24h: ShinyHunters exploited a critical zero-day in Oracle's PeopleSoft ERP to steal gigabytes from hundreds of organizations, hitting American universities hardest.

The flaw sits in Oracle-owned PeopleSoft, software that runs human resources and finance for large institutions, and Ars Technica rates it about as critical as vulnerabilities come. ShinyHunters capitalized fast, exfiltrating large troves of data from higher education in particular. Organizations running PeopleSoft should patch and hunt for signs of data theft immediately.

Sources: Ars Technica Security · Dark Reading

3. Decade-Old phpBB Auth Bypass

[patch, vulnerability]

Last 24h: phpBB patched a 10-year-old authentication bypass that let an attacker log in as any user, administrators included.

phpBB is widely deployed open-source forum software, so the flaw exposed countless community sites for years. The bypass grants full account takeover, including administrative control. Operators should apply the fix at once.

Sources: BleepingComputer

Ransomware and Cybercrime

4. Arch Linux AUR Hijack

[supply-chain, malware]

Last 24h: Attackers seized more than 400 packages in the Arch User Repository and rewrote their build scripts to drop a Rust infostealer and, with root, an eBPF rootkit.

The AUR is Arch Linux's community-maintained package collection, so any developer who builds a poisoned package runs the malware on their own machine. The Rust binary harvests developer secrets, credentials, and access tokens; when it lands with root it loads an eBPF rootkit to hide itself. Arch users should audit recently built AUR packages and rotate any exposed secrets.

Sources: The Hacker News · BleepingComputer

5. Google Sues Gemini-Abusing Smishing Network

[ai, phishing]

Last 24h: Google filed suit against a Chinese cybercrime network it accuses of weaponizing its Gemini AI agent to mass-produce phishing texts aimed at Americans.

Google ties the network to a phishing-as-a-service kit called Outsider, which it says used Gemini to help craft and send smishing messages. The legal action marks Google's attempt to disrupt the operation through the courts. The case underscores how criminals now fold consumer AI agents into commodity phishing toolkits.

Sources: The Hacker News

Nation-State Activity

6. Velvet Ant Linux Login Backdoor

[apt, china]

Last 24h: Sygnia reported that the China-nexus group Velvet Ant spent close to a decade inside a target's Linux login system, backdooring PAM and OpenSSH to survive ordinary cleanup.

Velvet Ant planted its access in the components that decide who may sign in, a layer defenders rarely inspect. The placement let the group persist for years where routine remediation could not reach. Defenders should verify the integrity of PAM modules and OpenSSH binaries on critical Linux hosts.

Sources: The Hacker News

Coupang joins a growing list of companies paying steeply for breach exposure. SecurityWeek flagged the penalty among the week's other notable items, alongside Google security layoffs and an AudiA6 takedown.

Sources: The Record · SecurityWeek

8. Section 702 Surveillance Lapse

[policy, surveillance]

Last 24h: A legislative deadlock left Section 702 of the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act poised to lapse, the program's first expiration since its 2008 passage.

Section 702 authorizes warrantless collection of foreign intelligence and underpins much US signals work. Congress failed to reach agreement before the deadline, so the authority faces its first gap in 18 years. The lapse raises questions over ongoing intelligence operations and the legal footing for collection already underway.

Sources: The Record
