AI Security

1. Anthropic Pulls Fable 5 and Mythos 5

[ai, policy, export-controls]

Last 24h: Anthropic disabled Claude Fable 5 and Mythos 5 worldwide after a government order at 5:21 p.m. ET to bar foreign nationals from the models.

The Trump administration ordered Anthropic to block all foreign nationals, inside or outside the U.S., from its most advanced models, citing national security and export controls. Anthropic responded by suspending both models for every user worldwide. The company complies yet disputes the rationale, calling the cited jailbreak narrow and the underlying capability common across competing models. Dark Reading notes Mythos 5 upgrades the earlier preview, and Fable 5 is the version Anthropic hardened for general use.

Sources: BleepingComputer · The Hacker News · SecurityWeek · Dark Reading

2. Google Sues Gemini-Powered Smishing Network

[ai, phishing, china]

Last 24h: Google filed suit against a Chinese cybercrime network it says weaponized its Gemini agent to mass-produce phishing texts aimed at Americans.

Google accuses the network of building and running a phishing-as-a-service kit called Outsider that leaned on Gemini to generate smishing messages targeting U.S. recipients. The suit escalates vendors' use of the courts against AI abuse. It also shows criminals folding mainstream AI agents into commodity fraud operations.

Sources: The Hacker News

Vulnerabilities and Exploits

3. Oracle PeopleSoft Zero-Day

[zero-day, breach]

Last 24h: ShinyHunters exploited an unpatched flaw in Oracle's PeopleSoft ERP to steal gigabytes of data from hundreds of organizations, hitting American universities hardest.

A critical zero-day in Oracle-owned PeopleSoft software lets attackers exfiltrate large volumes of data, and researchers rate it about as severe as bugs come. The extortion crew ShinyHunters seized on it, and higher-education institutions running the ERP suffered disproportionately. Organizations on PeopleSoft should hunt for signs of compromise and apply Oracle's fix the moment it ships.

Sources: Ars Technica Security · Dark Reading

4. Arch Linux AUR Hijack

[supply-chain, malware, linux]

Last 24h: Attackers took over more than 400 Arch User Repository packages and rewrote their build scripts to install a Rust infostealer and, with root, an eBPF rootkit.

Attackers compromised over 400 packages this week in the Arch User Repository, Arch Linux's community package collection. Each rewritten build script drops a Rust credential stealer that harvests developer secrets and access tokens on any machine that builds it, and when it gains root it loads an eBPF rootkit to hide. Developers who built AUR packages this week should rotate secrets and audit affected systems.

Sources: The Hacker News · BleepingComputer

Nation-State Activity

5. Velvet Ant Linux Login Backdoor

[apt, china, persistence]

Last 24h: Sygnia revealed that the China-nexus group Velvet Ant backdoored Linux PAM and OpenSSH components to hide inside the login system for nearly a decade.

The China-linked Velvet Ant group planted access deep inside the Linux authentication stack itself, the PAM and OpenSSH code that decides who may sign in. The placement let it survive routine cleanup and stay hidden close to ten years on a targeted network. Defenders should verify the integrity of PAM modules and SSH binaries on their most critical hosts.

Sources: The Hacker News

Data Breaches

6. Record Breach Penalties and Payouts

[breach, privacy, policy]

Last 24h: South Korea fined Coupang 409 million dollars over a data breach, its largest ever, while a bankruptcy administrator approved a 47 million dollar settlement for 23andMe breach victims.

South Korea's privacy commission levied a record 409 million dollar penalty on the e-commerce firm Coupang, eclipsing the 88.8 million dollar SK Telecom fine from earlier this year. A U.S. bankruptcy administrator separately approved a 47 million dollar fund for roughly 7 million 23andMe customers whose genetic data hackers stole starting in April 2023 and posted on the dark web. Together the actions mark mounting financial consequences for failing to protect personal data.

Sources: The Record · The Record · SecurityWeek

Policy and Regulation

7. Section 702 Surveillance Lapse

[policy, surveillance]

Last 24h: Section 702 of the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act is poised to lapse after a legislative deadlock, the first such lapse since its 2008 passage.

Congressional deadlock pushed the foreign-intelligence surveillance authority known as Section 702 toward expiration for the first time since 2008. Intelligence agencies rely on the program to collect communications of foreign targets abroad, a capability that reaches domestic providers and sweeps in Americans' incidental data. A lapse leaves that collection in legal limbo until lawmakers act.
