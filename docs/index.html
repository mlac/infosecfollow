Vulnerabilities and Exploits

1. Oracle PeopleSoft Zero-Day

[zero-day, breach]

Last 24h: The ShinyHunters crew exploited an Oracle PeopleSoft zero-day across hundreds of organizations, stealing gigabytes of data and hitting American universities hardest.

A critical flaw in Oracle's PeopleSoft ERP software lets attackers reach records without authentication. ShinyHunters capitalized fast, draining data from hundreds of victims, with higher-education institutions absorbing the worst of it. Organizations running PeopleSoft should apply Oracle's emergency fix and hunt for signs of bulk data export.

Sources: Dark Reading · Ars Technica Security

2. Arch Linux AUR Package Hijack

[supply-chain, malware, rootkit]

Last 24h: Attackers seized more than 400 packages in the Arch User Repository and rewrote their build scripts to drop a credential stealer and an eBPF rootkit.

The malware is a Rust binary that harvests developer secrets and access tokens from any machine that builds a poisoned package, and with root it loads an eBPF rootkit to hide itself. The AUR is Arch Linux's community-maintained collection, separate from the official repositories and unvetted by the project. The episode landed the same week npm announced that version 12 will stop running dependency scripts by default, a direct counter to this style of attack. Developers should rebuild affected machines and rotate every exposed credential.

Sources: The Hacker News · BleepingComputer · SecurityWeek

3. Critical Splunk Enterprise RCE

[patch, rce]

Last 24h: Splunk patched CVE-2026-20253, a 9.8-severity flaw that lets an unauthenticated attacker create or truncate files and run code remotely.

The bug affects Splunk Enterprise versions below 10.2.4 and 10.0.7, where an unauthenticated user can perform arbitrary file operations that escalate to remote code execution. Splunk's deep footprint inside security operations centers makes the platform a high-value target. Administrators should upgrade immediately.

Sources: The Hacker News

Nation-State Activity

4. Velvet Ant Decade-Long Login Backdoor

[apt, nation-state, backdoor]

Last 24h: Sygnia revealed that the China-linked Velvet Ant group hid inside a target's Linux login software for nearly ten years, controlling the entire authentication stack.

Velvet Ant backdoored the PAM and OpenSSH components that decide who signs in, planting access beyond the reach of routine cleanup. The group held persistence on an isolated network for roughly a decade with full view of administrator activity. Defenders should audit authentication binaries and login modules for tampering that endpoint tools miss.

Sources: BleepingComputer · The Hacker News

Policy and Regulation

5. Anthropic Pulls Fable 5 and Mythos 5

[policy, ai]

Last 24h: Anthropic took its most advanced models offline worldwide after the Trump administration ordered it to block all foreign nationals from Fable 5 and Mythos 5.

The government issued the order at 5:21 p.m. ET on national-security grounds, citing export controls and a jailbreak concern. Anthropic is complying but disputes the basis, calling the cited jailbreak narrow and the capability widely available elsewhere. The suspension hits every user worldwide, since the company could not cleanly separate foreign access, and marks the first time export rules have grounded a frontier AI model.

Sources: BleepingComputer · The Hacker News · SecurityWeek

6. Section 702 Surveillance Lapse

[policy, surveillance, privacy]

Last 24h: Section 702 of the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act is poised to lapse for the first time since 2008 after Congress deadlocked.

Section 702 authorizes warrantless collection of foreign communications and has drawn years of privacy fights over incidental capture of Americans' data. A legislative stalemate now threatens to suspend the program, a first in its history. Intelligence agencies warn of operational gaps; privacy advocates see leverage for reform.

Sources: The Record

[ai, phishing, cybercrime]

Last 24h: Google sued a Chinese cybercrime network it accuses of weaponizing its Gemini AI agent to mass-produce phishing texts aimed at Americans.

8. 23andMe Breach Settlement

[breach, settlement]

Last 24h: A bankruptcy administrator approved a $47 million fund to compensate victims of the 23andMe data breach.

Hackers stole data on about 7 million customers of the genetics-testing company starting in April 2023, and much of it surfaced on dark-web forums.
