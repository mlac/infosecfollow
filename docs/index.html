AI Security

1. U.S. Forces Anthropic to Suspend Fable 5 and Mythos 5

[ai, policy, export-controls]

Last 24h: Anthropic disabled Fable 5 and Mythos 5 for every user worldwide after a federal order arrived at 5:21 p.m. ET demanding it block foreign nationals.

The Trump administration directed Anthropic to bar all foreign nationals, inside the country or out, from its two most capable models, citing national security and a jailbreak. Anthropic complied and suspended both models globally, though it disputes the rationale, calling the cited jailbreak narrow and the capability common elsewhere. The move marks an aggressive use of export controls against a frontier AI vendor and strands paying customers overnight.

Vulnerabilities and Exploits

2. Oracle PeopleSoft Zero-Day Drives Mass Theft

[zero-day, breach, vulnerability]

Last 24h: Researchers detailed an Oracle PeopleSoft zero-day that ShinyHunters exploited to steal gigabytes from hundreds of organizations, hitting American universities hardest.

A critical flaw in Oracle's PeopleSoft ERP, widely deployed across higher education, let attackers reach data without authenticating. The ShinyHunters extortion crew capitalized, exfiltrating large troves from universities and other enterprises. Administrators running PeopleSoft should patch immediately and hunt for signs of bulk data theft.

Sources: Ars Technica Security · Dark Reading

3. Arch Linux AUR Packages Hijacked

[supply-chain, malware, linux]

Last 24h: Attackers hijacked more than 400 Arch User Repository packages, rewriting build scripts to drop a Rust credential stealer and an eBPF rootkit.

The Arch User Repository, a community collection separate from Arch Linux's official packages, builds software from user-supplied scripts. Attackers seized over 400 of them and inserted malware that harvests developer secrets and access tokens; with root it loads an eBPF rootkit to hide. Anyone who built an AUR package this week should rotate credentials and inspect for the rootkit.

Sources: The Hacker News · BleepingComputer

Nation-State Activity

4. Velvet Ant Backdoors Linux Login for a Decade

[apt, nation-state, backdoor]

Last 24h: Sygnia reported that China-nexus group Velvet Ant backdoored Linux PAM and OpenSSH login components and hid inside a target network for nearly a decade.

Velvet Ant planted its access in the software that authenticates logins, PAM and OpenSSH, where routine cleanup never reached. The group held persistence on the victim network for close to ten years. Defenders should audit authentication binaries and modules, not only the endpoints they watch most closely.

Data Breaches

7. Record Breach Penalties Hit Coupang and 23andMe

[breach, fine, policy]

Last 24h: South Korea fined Coupang a record 409 million dollars over a data breach, and a bankruptcy administrator approved a 47 million dollar settlement for 23andMe victims.

South Korea's commission levied its largest-ever data-breach penalty on the retailer Coupang, eclipsing the 88.8 million dollar fine against SK Telecom earlier this year. Separately, roughly 7 million 23andMe customers whose genetic data thieves stole beginning in April 2023 will share a 47 million dollar fund. Both cases show the mounting financial cost of failing to protect personal data.

Policy and Regulation

8. Section 702 Surveillance Authority Heads to Lapse

[policy, surveillance]

Last 24h: Section 702 of the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act heads toward its first lapse since 2008 as Congress deadlocks over reauthorization.

Section 702 lets U.S. intelligence agencies collect the communications of foreign targets abroad, sweeping in some Americans' data along the way. Legislative gridlock has pushed the program toward its first expiration since lawmakers passed it in 2008. A lapse would force agencies to wind down a major collection authority and would reignite the long fight over privacy and surveillance.
